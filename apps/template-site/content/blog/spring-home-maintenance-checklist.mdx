**TL;DR:** A Midwest winter beats up your house in four places: the roof, the HVAC system, the plumbing, and the electrical. Spring is the window to catch problems before they get expensive. Walk the roofline for missing shingles and clogged gutters, swap the furnace filter and book an AC tune-up, test outdoor spigots and the water heater, and check every GFCI and AFCI outlet. Most of this takes a Saturday morning.

---

If you live in Springfield, Madison, or anywhere between, your house just survived months of freeze-thaw cycles, ice dams, basement humidity, and a furnace that ran nonstop. Spring is when all that stress shows up. A solid spring home maintenance checklist is the cheapest insurance you can buy, because the problems you catch in May cost a fraction of what they cost in July. This guide walks through the same four areas a licensed contractor would inspect: roof, HVAC, plumbing, and electrical. Most of it you can do yourself in a morning. The rest is worth a phone call. The [National Association of Home Builders estimates](https://www.nahb.org/blog/2019/12/study-of-life-expectancy-of-home-components) most home systems have a clear, predictable lifespan, and routine maintenance is what gets you to the long end of that range.

## Why does spring matter more than any other season for home maintenance?

Spring is the only season that follows the harshest stress test your house gets all year. Freeze-thaw cycles flex roofing, expand water in pipes, and crack caulk. Catching that damage in April or May means you fix it before summer storms, heat waves, or a leaky pipe turn it into a bigger bill.

In the Midwest, the freeze-thaw count alone is brutal. Springfield and Madison both see well over 80 freeze-thaw cycles each winter, according to [climate data published by NOAA](https://www.ncei.noaa.gov/access/us-climate-normals/). Every one of those cycles works on your shingles, your siding, your concrete, and any water sitting in an outdoor pipe. Spring is also when ground moisture peaks, so basement leaks and slab issues finally show themselves. The earlier you find them, the cheaper they are to fix.

## What should you check on your roof in spring?

Walk the perimeter of your house from the ground and look for four things: missing or curled shingles, granules in the gutters or downspouts, sagging or detached gutters, and any flashing that has lifted around chimneys or vents. You do not need to get on the roof. Most damage from a Midwest winter is visible from the lawn with a pair of binoculars.

Ice dams are the big winter villain in this region. When attic heat melts snow on the upper roof and it refreezes at the eaves, water backs up under the shingles and finds the decking. The [University of Minnesota Extension's ice dam guidance](https://extension.umn.edu/protecting-home-yard-and-garden-cold/dealing-ice-dams) explains the mechanism well. By spring, the visible damage looks like stained ceilings near exterior walls, peeling paint at the eaves, or shingles that lifted and never laid back down. The [National Roofing Contractors Association recommends](https://www.nrca.net/homeowners) a professional roof inspection at least once a year, and after winter is the right time for it.

Gutters do more than catch rain. Clogged or detached gutters dump water against the foundation and refreeze into ice dams the following winter. Clear them out, run a hose through them, and make sure downspouts carry water at least four feet away from the house. If you see shingle granules piling up in the gutters or at the bottom of downspouts, your roof is shedding its protective layer, and that is a sign it is approaching the end of its life. For a deeper look at timing a replacement, see our guide to [how long a roof actually lasts](/blog/how-long-does-a-roof-last) and [how to spot a roof leak early](/blog/how-to-spot-a-roof-leak-early).

## What is the spring HVAC checklist?

Replace the filter, vacuum out the supply registers, clear at least two feet of space around the outdoor AC unit, and book a professional AC tune-up before the first 90 degree day. Heat pump and central AC systems both lose efficiency when they are dirty or low on refrigerant, so a yearly check pays for itself in lower utility bills.

The filter is the single biggest variable in how hard your system has to work. [Energy Star recommends](https://www.energystar.gov/products/heating_cooling/change_air_filter) changing it every one to three months during heavy use. After a winter of constant furnace runtime, yours is probably overdue. While you are at it, vacuum the supply and return registers and check that no furniture is blocking airflow.

Outside, your condenser unit spent the winter collecting leaves, mulch, and the occasional small animal. Hose it down gently with the power off, clear two feet of space around it, and make sure it sits level. Then book a pro. A real tune-up should follow the procedures in [ACCA Standard 4 for quality HVAC maintenance](https://www.acca.org/standards/approved-standards), and a good technician will check refrigerant charge, electrical contacts, the capacitor, and blower amp draw. Our breakdown of [what an HVAC tune-up actually includes](/blog/what-is-an-hvac-tune-up) walks through the full checklist so you know what you are paying for. If your furnace struggled this winter, the [furnace troubleshooting guide](/blog/furnace-not-heating-troubleshooting) is worth a read before you forget the symptoms.

## How do you spring-check your plumbing after a Midwest winter?

Test every outdoor spigot for slow flow or leaks at the wall, flush the water heater, check supply lines under sinks for any sign of moisture, and walk the basement looking for stains on the floor or efflorescence on the walls. Frozen pipes do not always burst right away. Sometimes they crack and leak slowly once the thaw is done.

Outdoor spigots are the most common spring surprise. Turn each one on full and have someone inside listen for water hissing inside the wall. If you hear water but the spigot drips weakly, you likely have a split pipe from a hard freeze. Our piece on [how to prevent frozen pipes](/blog/how-to-prevent-frozen-pipes) covers the steps for next winter. For finding a slow drip you cannot see, [how to find a hidden water leak](/blog/how-to-find-a-hidden-water-leak) walks through the meter test and other diagnostics.

Water heaters need a yearly flush to clear out sediment, which is worse in hard-water areas like central Illinois and southern Wisconsin. The [U.S. Department of Energy's water heater guidance](https://www.energy.gov/energysaver/water-heating) explains why sediment cuts efficiency and shortens the tank's life. While you are down there, confirm the temperature is set somewhere in the 120 degree range, which the [CPSC recommends](https://www.cpsc.gov/Safety-Education/Safety-Guides/Home/Tap-Water-Scalds) for scald prevention. Our guide on [the right water heater temperature setting](/blog/water-heater-temperature-setting) goes deeper. Finally, scan your basement for new stains, white powdery residue on the walls (efflorescence), or musty smells. Any of those means water is finding its way in.

## What electrical checks belong on the spring list?

Push the test button on every GFCI and AFCI outlet in the house, replace any that do not trip, check outdoor outlets and lighting for moisture damage, and look at your panel for corrosion, rust, or warm-feeling breakers. Outdoor electrical takes a beating in winter, and a five-minute test once a year catches most of what goes wrong.

GFCI outlets (the ones with the test and reset buttons) protect against shock in wet areas. AFCI breakers protect against arc faults that start fires. Both are required in most areas of a modern home under the [National Electrical Code](https://www.nfpa.org/codes-and-standards/nfpa-70-standard-development/70), and both wear out. Press test, confirm the outlet kills power, and press reset. If nothing happens on either side, the device is bad and needs to be replaced. The [Electrical Safety Foundation International's home checklist](https://www.esfi.org/home-electrical-safety/) is a good reference.

Outdoor outlets, deck lighting, and exterior fixtures should all have weatherproof covers that close properly. Open one up after a wet spring week and you may find standing water inside. While you are at it, open the panel door (do not remove the cover) and look for rust streaks, scorch marks, or any breaker that feels warm to the touch. Those are signs to call a licensed electrician. If your home is more than 30 years old or you have been adding loads (a hot tub, an EV charger, a heat pump), it may be time to read about [the signs your electrical panel needs an upgrade](/blog/signs-your-electrical-panel-needs-an-upgrade).

## What can you safely do yourself and what should you leave to a pro?

DIY safely: filter changes, gutter cleaning from a stable ladder, hosing off the AC condenser, testing GFCI and AFCI outlets, flushing the water heater, and a visual inspection of the roof from the ground. Call a pro for: roof repairs, refrigerant work, panel inspections, gas appliance service, and anything that involves opening up walls or climbing on a steep roof.

The line is roughly this. Anything that needs a license, a permit, or specialty tools belongs to a licensed contractor. Anything you can do with a screwdriver, a vacuum, and good shoes is fair game. The [Consumer Product Safety Commission](https://www.cpsc.gov/) tracks home injuries every year, and ladder falls and electrical shocks consistently top the list. A spring checklist is not worth a trip to the ER.

When you do hire someone, get an itemized quote, ask about licensing and insurance, and skip anyone going door to door. ## Frequently asked questions

### When should I start my spring home maintenance in the Midwest?

As soon as overnight temperatures stay reliably above freezing, usually mid-March in central Illinois and early April in southern Wisconsin. Starting before the last hard freeze means risking a refreeze on the outdoor plumbing tests. Most homeowners finish the bulk of the list across two weekends in April.

### How much does a full spring inspection cost if I hire it out?

Expect $80 to $200 for an HVAC tune-up per system, $0 to $300 for a roof inspection (many reputable roofers do them free for existing customers), and $100 to $250 for an electrical safety inspection. A plumber's visual walkthrough usually runs $100 to $200. Bundling with a maintenance plan often saves 10 to 20 percent.

### Do I really need an annual AC tune-up if the system seems fine?

Yes, for two reasons. [Energy Star](https://www.energystar.gov/saveathome/heating_cooling/maintenance) notes that dirty coils and low refrigerant can cut efficiency by 5 to 15 percent, and most manufacturers require documented annual maintenance to keep the warranty valid. A $100 tune-up that prevents a $400 utility bill spike pays for itself by July.

### What is the most commonly missed item on a spring checklist?

Testing GFCI and AFCI outlets. They are designed to fail safe, but if the internal mechanism wears out, the outlet still delivers power without the protection. Most homeowners have never pressed the test button, which is why the [ESFI](https://www.esfi.org/home-electrical-safety/) calls it the single highest-value five-minute check in the house.

### Should I clean my own gutters or hire it out?

If you have a single-story house, a stable ladder, and a helper, cleaning gutters yourself is reasonable. For two-story homes, steep yards, or anyone uncomfortable on a ladder, hire it out. Gutter cleaning typically runs $100 to $250, and a pro will also flag any sagging, separated, or rusting sections you might miss.
