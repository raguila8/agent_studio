**TL;DR:** After a storm, walk the perimeter of your house and look for missing or lifted shingles, dented gutters, and granules in downspouts. Then check the attic for daylight, water stains, or damp insulation. Take time-stamped photos for your insurance company, file within your policy's deadline (often 12 months but sometimes shorter), and skip anyone going door to door offering a "free roof inspection." Call a licensed local roofer for anything you can't safely see from the ground.

---

Storms move fast, and roof damage usually doesn't announce itself. A few lifted shingles or a hairline crack around a vent can sit quietly for months before you notice a stain on the ceiling. By then, the wet insulation and rotted decking cost a lot more to fix than the original problem. This roof storm damage checklist walks you through what to inspect, in what order, and how to document everything so your insurance claim goes smoothly. You don't need to climb a ladder. Most of what matters can be spotted from the ground or your attic, and the rest is a job for a licensed roofer.

## What kind of storm damage should you look for on a roof?

The four big categories are wind, hail, debris, and water intrusion. Wind lifts or tears shingles. Hail bruises shingles and dents soft metal. Debris (branches, blown shingles from a neighbor's house) punctures or scrapes the surface. Water finds any weak spot the first three left behind.

The [National Roofing Contractors Association](https://www.nrca.net/) notes that even mid-strength thunderstorms can break the seal on asphalt shingles without tearing them off, which leaves them vulnerable to the next storm. That's why a quick check after every major weather event matters, not just the ones that knock down fences. The [Insurance Institute for Business & Home Safety](https://ibhs.org/) has shown in lab testing that wind speeds as low as 50 mph can start lifting older shingles, and gusts in that range are common in any thunderstorm.

If you're not sure how bad the storm actually was in your zip code, the [NOAA Storm Events Database](https://www.ncdc.noaa.gov/stormevents/) lets you search by date and county to see recorded wind speeds, hail size, and damage reports. Insurance adjusters use the same data. It's worth a one-minute search before you file a claim.

## Start with a ground-level walkaround

Before anything else, walk slowly around the outside of your house and look up. You're not trying to spot every problem from down there. You're scanning for the obvious stuff so you know whether the damage is minor or something bigger.

Look for these from the ground:

- Shingles in the yard, driveway, or flower beds
- Missing patches on the roof (you'll see a darker rectangle where the underlayment shows through)
- Shingles that look curled, lifted, or out of alignment
- Dents or dings in gutters, downspouts, vents, or flashing
- A pile of asphalt granules at the bottom of downspouts (looks like coarse black sand)
- Cracked or shifted ridge caps along the peak
- Damaged or bent vent pipes, chimney caps, and satellite dishes

A garden hose's worth of granules at the bottom of a downspout is normal for an older roof. A coffee-can full after a storm is not. Granule loss exposes the asphalt mat underneath to UV, and once that starts, the shingle's life shortens fast.

Use your phone to take wide photos of each side of the house, then zoom in on anything that looks off. You'll want these later.

## How do you check for roof damage without going on the roof?

You can spot most storm damage from three places: the ground around your house, your attic, and a window or upstairs balcony with a roof view. Use binoculars or your phone's zoom for the roof itself. Climbing up after a storm is dangerous, especially on a wet or sloped roof, and most issues show up just as clearly from below.

[FEMA's homeowner guidance](https://www.fema.gov/) on post-storm inspections is clear about this: leave actual roof walking to a licensed professional with fall protection. Insurance companies don't expect you to climb up either. A clear set of zoomed photos and an attic inspection is enough for an initial claim.

If you have a drone and know how to fly it, that's another safe way to get close-up shots. Just don't fly over a neighbor's property without asking.

## Go into the attic with a flashlight

The attic tells you things the outside can't. Even if the shingles look fine, water might already be working its way in. Pick a clear, dry day, grab a flashlight, and head up.

Look for:

- **Daylight coming through the roof deck.** Any pinhole of light is a leak waiting to happen.
- **Dark stains on the underside of the plywood or rafters.** Old stains are dry and brown. Fresh ones are dark and may still feel damp.
- **Wet or matted insulation.** Push it aside gently to check the joists underneath.
- **Mold or a musty smell**, especially near the eaves or around chimneys and vent pipes.
- **Rust on nails poking through the deck.** Rust means moisture is condensing or leaking around those nails.
- **Sagging or bowing of the roof deck**, which can mean water has been soaking it for a while.

Take photos of anything you find. If you smell mold or see active dripping, that's an emergency, not a "next week" problem. Stop and call a roofer the same day.

## How long do you have to file a roof insurance claim after a storm?

Most homeowner policies require you to report storm damage within 12 months of the event, but many companies push for notice within 30 to 60 days. Your specific deadline is in your policy under "duties after loss." Miss it, and the insurer can deny the claim even if the damage is real. Check your policy before you do anything else.

The [Insurance Information Institute](https://www.iii.org/) recommends calling your insurance company as soon as you suspect damage, even if you haven't had a roofer look at it yet. Filing a claim and pursuing a claim are different steps. Reporting early protects your deadline. You can decide later whether to move forward based on the estimate and your deductible.

When you call, you'll need:

- The date and approximate time of the storm
- A short description of what you noticed (lifted shingles, leak in the kitchen ceiling, dents in the gutters)
- Your policy number
- Photos, if you have them

The adjuster will schedule an inspection, usually within a week or two depending on how many claims are open in your area.

## How to document roof damage for an insurance claim

Documentation is what separates a smooth claim from a frustrating one. Adjusters look at evidence, not stories. The more you have, before anything gets repaired, the better.

Here's what to capture:

1. **Wide shots of each side of the house** showing the overall roof.
2. **Close-ups of damaged areas** with something for scale (a quarter works well for hail dents).
3. **Photos of debris on the ground**, including fallen shingles, branches, and granule piles.
4. **Interior photos** of any water stains, wet insulation, or sagging drywall.
5. **A note of the date and time** of the storm and when you took the photos. Most phones embed this automatically, but write it down too.
6. **Receipts** for any emergency repairs you make to prevent further damage (tarping, plywood). Insurance usually reimburses these if the claim is approved.

The [Insurance Information Institute's claims guide](https://www.iii.org/article/how-to-file-a-homeowners-claim) recommends keeping a written log of every conversation with the insurance company, including the date, the person's name, and what was discussed. It sounds like overkill until you need it.

Don't throw away damaged materials until the adjuster has seen them. A pile of broken shingles in the yard is evidence.

## Why "free roof inspections" after a storm can be a red flag

After every major storm, traveling contractors (often called "storm chasers") flood the area knocking on doors and offering free roof inspections. Some are legitimate. Many are not. The [Federal Trade Commission](https://consumer.ftc.gov/) and several state attorneys general have published warnings about this pattern for years.

The scam usually goes like this: someone climbs up, comes back down, and says you have major damage. They offer to "handle the insurance" for you, ask you to sign an "assignment of benefits" form, and start the claim themselves. Sometimes they manufacture damage to make the claim stick. Sometimes they take a deposit and disappear. Sometimes they do shoddy work and skip town before your warranty matters.

A few warning signs:

- Out-of-state license plates or no permanent local address
- High-pressure tactics ("we need to act today")
- Asking you to sign anything before you've called your insurance company
- Refusing to give a written estimate with their license number
- Offering to "waive" or "absorb" your deductible (this is illegal in most states)

Stick with a licensed local roofer who has a verifiable address, real reviews, and a name that's been in your area for years. If you're not sure where to start, our [roofing services page](/services/roofing) explains how we approach storm damage inspections and what to expect during the visit.

## When should you call a professional roofer?

Call a licensed roofer if you see any of these: missing shingles, visible daylight in the attic, active dripping, sagging, a tree on the roof, or hail dents on metal surfaces. Also call if you're just not sure. A 20-minute professional inspection costs less than a missed leak that ruins drywall and insulation.

A good roofer will get on the roof safely, document what they find with photos, and give you a written report. If insurance is involved, they should also be willing to meet the adjuster on site. That joint visit is one of the most useful things you can arrange. Two trained eyes looking at the same damage tend to produce a more accurate estimate than either alone.

## Frequently asked questions

### How can I tell if hail actually damaged my roof?

Hail damage on asphalt shingles looks like dark, round bruises where the granules have been knocked off. Run your hand gently over the spot and you'll often feel a soft dent. On metal flashing, gutters, or vents, you'll see clear round dimples. Hail under one inch can damage older shingles, while newer impact-rated shingles often handle up to 1.5 inches. The [Insurance Institute for Business & Home Safety](https://ibhs.org/) maintains testing data on impact ratings if you want to check your specific shingle type.

### Will my insurance pay for a new roof after a storm?

It depends on the damage, your policy, and your deductible. Most policies cover sudden storm damage but not wear and tear or pre-existing problems. If the storm caused enough damage that repair isn't reasonable, many insurers will pay for a full replacement, minus your deductible. Older roofs (typically 15+ years) sometimes get paid out at "actual cash value" rather than full replacement cost, so check your policy's depreciation terms.

### How soon after a storm should I inspect my roof?

As soon as it's safe to walk outside, do the ground-level walkaround. Wait until the roof is dry before doing anything more involved, and call a professional before climbing up. The sooner you document damage, the easier the insurance claim. Small leaks left for weeks can soak insulation and lead to mold, so don't put it off.

### Can I file an insurance claim if I don't see obvious damage but my neighbor did?

Yes. If the storm hit your block, your roof was exposed to the same conditions. Damage isn't always visible from the ground. Many homeowners discover lifted shingles or broken seals only after a roofer takes a close look. If you're inside the same storm radius and your neighbor's claim was approved, it's worth getting an inspection.

### Do I need three estimates before filing a claim?

No. Your insurance company assigns its own adjuster who creates the official damage estimate. You only need one estimate from a licensed contractor, ideally one who can meet the adjuster on site. Some homeowners get a second opinion if they disagree with the adjuster's number, but three estimates isn't required and can slow things down.
