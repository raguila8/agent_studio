**TL;DR:** If your home still has a fuse box, a 60 or 100 amp service, a panel from a recalled brand like Federal Pacific or Zinsco, frequent breaker trips, warm or scorched panel parts, no room for new circuits, or plans for an EV charger or heat pump, your electrical panel needs an upgrade. A 200 amp replacement typically runs $2,000 to $4,500 with permits and inspection.

---

Your electrical panel is the quiet workhorse of your home. It splits incoming power into the circuits that feed every outlet, light, and appliance. When the panel is healthy, you forget it exists. When it isn't, you get flickering lights, tripped breakers, and in the worst cases, a real fire risk.

Older panels were built for a different era. A 1970s home ran a fridge, a TV, and maybe a window AC. Today the same house might charge an EV in the garage, run a heat pump, and power a home office full of electronics. The [U.S. Department of Energy's electrification guidance](https://www.energy.gov/energysaver/home-electrification) makes it clear: home electrical loads are climbing fast, and many older panels just weren't designed for them.

Here are seven signs your panel is ready to retire, what an upgrade actually involves, and why doing it right matters.

## Sign 1: you still have a fuse box

If you replace screw-in fuses instead of flipping breakers, your panel is almost certainly 50+ years old. Fuse boxes aren't inherently dangerous, but they're a strong signal that the rest of your electrical system, the wiring, the grounding, and the service capacity, is also from another era.

Most home insurers now flag fuse boxes during underwriting, and some won't write a new policy until the panel is replaced. The [Electrical Safety Foundation International (ESFI)](https://www.esfi.org/) notes that aging electrical systems are a leading cause of home electrical fires, and fuse boxes are at the top of the aging-systems list.

## Sign 2: your panel is a Federal Pacific, Zinsco, or other recalled brand

This is the big one. If your panel is a Federal Pacific Electric (FPE) "Stab-Lok," a Zinsco, a Sylvania-Zinsco, or a Challenger, you have a panel with a documented history of breakers that fail to trip during overloads or short circuits. A breaker that doesn't trip is a breaker that lets a fault keep burning.

The [Consumer Product Safety Commission has investigated FPE panels](https://www.cpsc.gov/) for decades, and home inspectors at [InterNACHI](https://www.nachi.org/fpe-panels.htm) recommend replacement regardless of whether the panel appears to be working. If you see one of these brand names on your panel cover, get an electrician out to confirm and quote a replacement. This isn't a "wait and see" situation.

## Sign 3: do you only have 60 or 100 amp service?

If your main breaker is rated 60 or 100 amps, your panel is undersized for a modern home. A 60 amp service was standard in homes built before the 1960s. A 100 amp service became typical from the 1960s through the 1980s. Today, [200 amps is the baseline](https://www.energystar.gov/) for new construction in most of the country, and 400 amps is increasingly common for all-electric homes.

You can read your service size off the main breaker at the top of the panel. If it says "60" or "100" and you've added central air, a hot tub, an electric range, or are even thinking about an EV, you're already at or over capacity. Upgrading to 200 amps gives you headroom for the next 20 years of additions.

## Sign 4: why do my breakers keep tripping?

Occasional trips are normal. A breaker that trips every time you run the microwave and toaster together, or pops on its own with nothing obvious running, is telling you something is wrong. The cause is usually one of three things: a circuit that's chronically overloaded, a worn breaker that trips below its rated amperage, or a panel that's simply too small for the load.

If you're already troubleshooting frequent trips, our guide on [why a circuit breaker keeps tripping](/blog/circuit-breaker-keeps-tripping) walks through what to check first. But if multiple breakers in the panel trip regularly, the panel itself is the problem, not the individual circuits.

## Sign 5: the panel is warm, smells like burning, or has scorch marks

Open the panel cover (or have an electrician do it) and look at the breakers and bus bars. Discoloration, melted plastic, scorch marks, or a faint burning smell are emergency-level warnings. So is a panel that feels warm to the touch on the outside.

These signs point to loose connections, arcing, or overheated wiring inside the enclosure. The [National Fire Protection Association reports](https://www.nfpa.org/News-Blogs-and-Articles/Blogs/2022/12/01/Electrical-Fires) that electrical distribution and lighting equipment is involved in roughly 13% of home structure fires each year. If you see any of these signs, stop using high-load appliances and call an electrician the same day.

## Sign 6: you can't add new circuits because the panel is full

Every breaker slot in your panel is in use. You want to add a circuit for a new bathroom, a workshop outlet, or an EV charger, and there's literally nowhere to put it. Some electricians will suggest "tandem" or "double-stuff" breakers to squeeze two circuits into one slot, but that's a workaround, not a fix, and many panels aren't rated for them.

A full panel is the most common reason homeowners discover they need an upgrade. You don't notice the problem until you try to add something. If your panel is full and you're planning any project that needs new circuits, plan the panel upgrade first.

## Sign 7: you're planning an EV charger, heat pump, or induction range

This is where the math gets serious. A Level 2 EV charger pulls 40 to 50 amps. A heat pump can pull 30 to 60 amps depending on size and climate. An induction range adds another 40 amps. A heat pump water heater adds 30. Stack two or three of these on a 100 amp panel, and you're past capacity before you turn anything on.

The [DOE's home electrification resources](https://www.energy.gov/energysaver/home-electrification) recommend a load calculation before adding any major electrified appliance. An electrician can do this in about an hour. If the calc shows you're close to your service limit, upgrade the panel first. Doing it the other way around (adding the appliance, then discovering the panel can't handle it) costs more and creates safety risk in the meantime.

## What does a panel upgrade actually cost?

A standard 200 amp panel upgrade in most U.S. markets runs $2,000 to $4,500 for the panel itself, the labor, the permit, and the inspection. Costs go up if the meter base needs replacing, if the service entrance cable from the utility has to be upsized, or if the panel location is moving. Whole-home rewiring is a separate project and a separate cost.

Cheap quotes are a red flag. A licensed electrician pulling a permit and scheduling an inspection has to cover their license, insurance, and the time the inspector takes. Anyone offering to do a panel "off the books" is also skipping the inspection that catches mistakes before they become house fires.

## Why permits and inspections matter

A panel upgrade is the one electrical project where cutting corners shows up later as a real problem. The [National Electrical Code (NFPA 70)](https://www.nfpa.org/codes-and-standards/nfpa-70) is updated every three years, and any new panel work has to meet the current code in your jurisdiction. That includes things like AFCI and GFCI protection on specific circuits, proper grounding and bonding, and labeling.

A permitted job means a third-party inspector verifies the work meets code. If you ever sell the house, unpermitted electrical work can kill the deal during the buyer's inspection, or force you to redo the project with a permit before closing. The permit fee (usually $50 to $200) is cheap insurance.

## How long does a panel upgrade take?

Most residential panel upgrades take one day. The electrician coordinates with the utility to cut power at the meter in the morning, swaps the panel and any required service-entrance components, gets the inspector out the same day or next, and has power restored by evening. You'll lose power for 6 to 8 hours.

Larger jobs (service size increases that need utility upgrades, meter relocations, or panels in awkward spots) can stretch to two or three days. Your electrician should give you a realistic window in writing before the job starts. ## Frequently asked questions

### How do I tell if my panel is a Federal Pacific or Zinsco?

Open the panel door and look at the breaker labels and the manufacturer name printed on the inside of the door or the deadfront. Federal Pacific panels say "FPE" or "Stab-Lok." Zinsco panels often show the Zinsco or Sylvania-Zinsco logo and have distinctive colored breaker handles (red, blue, green). If you're not sure, take a photo of the inside of the panel and show it to a licensed electrician.

### Can I upgrade my own electrical panel?

In almost every jurisdiction, no. Panel work requires a licensed electrician because it involves the utility's service drop, and most utilities won't reconnect power to unpermitted work. Even where DIY is technically allowed, the [ESFI](https://www.esfi.org/) and most home insurers strongly discourage it. The risk of a wiring mistake inside a live service is too high.

### Will a panel upgrade lower my insurance premium?

Often, yes. Replacing a fuse box or a recalled-brand panel typically removes an underwriting flag that some insurers apply to older electrical systems. Call your insurance agent before and after the upgrade and ask. Some homeowners see $100 to $300 a year in savings, plus the broader benefit of being insurable at all.

### Does a panel upgrade increase home value?

It doesn't add dollar-for-dollar value the way a kitchen remodel might, but it removes a major deduction from your home's appraisal and inspection report. Buyers and their inspectors flag old panels, and a recent upgrade with a closed permit on file is a clean checkmark. For all-electric or solar-ready homes, a 200 amp panel is essentially required.

### What's the difference between a panel upgrade and a service upgrade?

A panel upgrade replaces the breaker box itself. A service upgrade increases the amperage coming from the utility (say, from 100 to 200 amps), which usually involves replacing the meter base, the service-entrance cable, and the panel together. Most "panel upgrades" homeowners ask about are actually full service upgrades. Your electrician will confirm which you need based on your current setup.
