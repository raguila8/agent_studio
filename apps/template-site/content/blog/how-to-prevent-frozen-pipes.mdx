**TL;DR:** Pipes freeze when cold air reaches stagnant water, and wind exposure matters as much as temperature. To prevent frozen pipes in a Midwest winter, insulate any pipe in an unheated space, seal rim joist gaps, keep your thermostat at 55 degrees or higher even when you travel, open cabinet doors during deep cold, and let a faucet on an exterior wall drip when temperatures drop below zero. If a pipe does freeze, shut off the main water valve before you thaw it.

---

A frozen pipe is one of those winter problems that feels minor right up until the moment it is not. A pipe can sit frozen for a day or two with no obvious sign, and then split the second the ice expands past what the metal or plastic can take. By the time you hear water hitting the basement floor, you are looking at drywall, flooring, and a few thousand dollars of damage.

This matters more in Springfield and Madison than almost anywhere else. The [National Weather Service Quad Cities office reports average January lows in central Illinois sitting around 17 degrees](https://www.weather.gov/dvn/climate), and [Madison's January low averages roughly 11 degrees with arctic outbreaks pushing well below zero](https://www.weather.gov/mkx/climate). That is plenty cold enough to freeze any pipe the cold air can reach.

The good news is that frozen pipes are almost always preventable. This guide walks through why pipes freeze in the first place, the prevention steps that actually work, what to do if you find a frozen pipe, and when to stop and call a plumber.

## Why do pipes freeze in the first place?

Pipes freeze when three things line up: water sitting still inside the pipe, cold air reaching the pipe, and enough time for that water to drop below 32 degrees. Wind exposure speeds this up dramatically. A pipe in a wall facing a 20 mph north wind freezes faster than the same pipe at the same temperature on a calm day.

Most people assume the trigger is just the outside temperature. It is not. The [Insurance Institute for Business and Home Safety found that the freezing point inside an uninsulated wall cavity can be reached when outdoor temperatures hold around 20 degrees, especially with wind](https://disastersafety.org/freezing/frozen-pipes/). Stagnant water is the other piece. Moving water freezes much more slowly than still water, which is why the old advice to drip a faucet during a cold snap actually works.

In practice, the pipes that freeze in Midwest homes are almost always in the same places: exterior wall cavities, unheated basements and crawl spaces, attics, garages, and pipes that run through a rim joist where insulation is thin or missing.

## What temperature do pipes start to freeze at?

Pipes can start to freeze when the air around them drops to about 20 degrees Fahrenheit, not 32. The buffer comes from the heat of the surrounding building. Once that buffer disappears, due to a power outage, an open garage door, or a wind-driven cold front, freezing can happen within a few hours.

This is why your thermostat setting matters even when you are away. The [American Red Cross recommends keeping your home at 55 degrees or higher, day and night, including when you travel](https://www.redcross.org/get-help/how-to-prepare-for-emergencies/types-of-emergencies/winter-storm/frozen-pipes.html). Saving a few dollars on heat over a long weekend is not worth the risk of coming home to a flooded basement.

In Madison and Springfield, multi-day stretches below zero are not unusual. During those stretches, even well-built homes can lose enough interior wall temperature to put pipes at risk. The fix is not turning the heat up to 80. It is making sure heat actually reaches the spaces where pipes live.

## Which pipes are most likely to freeze?

The pipes most likely to freeze are the ones running through unheated or poorly insulated spaces: exterior walls, attics, basements, crawl spaces, garages, and rim joists. Outdoor spigots and any pipe feeding a hose bib are at the top of the list. Inside the house, kitchen and bathroom sinks on exterior walls come next.

If you have ever opened the cabinet under a kitchen sink on an exterior wall and felt cold air, you have found one of the most common freeze points in a Midwest house. The drain and supply lines are sitting just inches from outdoor air, with a thin wall and maybe a little fiberglass insulation between them and a sub-zero night.

Older homes are more vulnerable because insulation standards have changed. A house built in the 1960s likely has uninsulated rim joists, single-pane windows nearby, and supply lines stapled directly to exterior framing. None of that was a problem when the heat was running full blast. It becomes a problem when the heat is set back, the wind picks up, or the power goes out.

## How do you insulate pipes to prevent freezing?

To insulate pipes, slide closed-cell foam pipe sleeves over any exposed pipe in an unheated space, then seal the seams with foil tape. For pipes in extreme cold, wrap with UL-listed heat trace cable on a thermostat, then add the foam sleeve over the top. Air sealing matters as much as insulation: caulk any gap that lets outside air touch the pipe.

Foam pipe sleeves are sold at every hardware store in five and six foot lengths. They cost a few dollars per pipe and install in seconds. The trick most homeowners miss is sealing the seam. Foam tape or foil tape across the slit keeps cold air from sneaking past the insulation.

For pipes that have frozen before, heat trace cable is worth the extra effort. The [Plumbing-Heating-Cooling Contractors Association recommends UL-listed heat tape sized to the pipe, installed per manufacturer instructions, and never doubled over itself](https://www.phccweb.org/). Plug it into a GFCI outlet and let the built-in thermostat handle the rest.

Do not forget the rim joist. The [Department of Energy notes that air leaks at the rim joist are one of the biggest sources of heat loss in basements](https://www.energy.gov/energysaver/air-sealing-your-home), and they are exactly where supply pipes often run. A bead of caulk and a piece of rigid foam can drop that area from freezing-cold to safe.

## Do faucet drips really prevent frozen pipes?

Yes. A slow drip from a faucet on an exterior wall keeps water moving through the pipe, which raises the temperature at which freezing would actually happen. It also relieves the pressure that builds up between an ice blockage and a closed faucet, which is what usually causes pipes to burst, not the ice itself.

You do not need a stream. A pencil-thin trickle from the faucet farthest from where water enters the house is enough. The [American Red Cross specifically recommends letting cold water drip from a faucet served by exposed pipes during extreme cold](https://www.redcross.org/get-help/how-to-prepare-for-emergencies/types-of-emergencies/winter-storm/frozen-pipes.html).

This is a short-term tactic, not a year-round one. Use it when temperatures are forecast to drop below zero, when wind chills hit the negative teens, or when you have had a freeze in that location before. Open the cabinet doors under sinks on exterior walls at the same time so warm room air can reach the pipes behind the cabinet.

## What should you do before you leave town in winter?

Before you leave town in winter, set the thermostat no lower than 55 degrees, shut off the main water valve if you will be gone more than a few days, drain outdoor spigots, and ask a neighbor to check the house. If you have a smart thermostat, set a low-temperature alert so you get a phone notification if the house drops below 50.

The biggest mistake people make is setting the heat back to 45 or 50 to save on a utility bill. The [Insurance Information Institute warns that a single burst pipe can release hundreds of gallons of water per hour and that frozen pipe claims average well over $10,000](https://www.iii.org/article/preventing-and-thawing-frozen-pipes). The savings on heat are not in the same universe.

A pre-trip checklist that takes 10 minutes:

- Set thermostat to 55 degrees or higher.
- Open cabinet doors under sinks on exterior walls.
- Drip one faucet at the end of the supply run if a cold snap is forecast.
- Disconnect any garden hose still attached to a spigot.
- Shut off and drain the line to outdoor spigots if you have an interior shutoff.
- Give a neighbor a key and your phone number.

These are the same steps insurance carriers ask about after a claim. Doing them is also what most policies quietly expect you to do as a homeowner.

## What do you do if a pipe freezes?

If a pipe freezes, shut off the main water valve to the house first, then open the affected faucet so melting water has somewhere to go. Thaw the pipe from the faucet end back toward the frozen section using a hair dryer, heating pad, or space heater kept a safe distance from anything flammable. Never use an open flame, propane torch, or charcoal heater.

Shutting off the water before you thaw is the step most people skip. If the pipe has already split, you will not know until the ice melts and water starts pouring out. Having the main shut off means you control when the water flows, not the broken pipe.

Work from the faucet back, not the middle out. If you heat the middle of a frozen section first, melt water has nowhere to go and the pressure can push past the ice block into a weak spot. Slow and steady, starting where the water needs to exit, is the safe way.

[FEMA's frozen pipe guidance explicitly warns against blowtorches, kerosene heaters, charcoal stoves, or any device with an open flame](https://www.ready.gov/winter-weather), both because of fire risk and because intense local heat can crack the pipe. A $20 hair dryer is the right tool for this job.

## When should you call a plumber?

Call a plumber if you cannot locate the frozen section, if the pipe is inside a wall, if you find any wet drywall or ceiling staining, or if water keeps running after you close the main valve. Also call if multiple pipes are frozen at once, which usually means a larger heating or insulation problem the house needs solved before the next cold night.

A frozen pipe inside a wall is not a DIY situation. Cutting drywall, locating the freeze, thawing without overheating the framing, and then repairing the line takes tools and experience most homeowners do not have at 10pm on a Saturday in February. The [American Water Works Association notes that delayed response to a frozen or burst pipe dramatically increases the cost of damage](https://www.awwa.org/). The longer the water sits, the more flooring, drywall, and framing it ruins.

## Frequently asked questions

### How cold does it have to be for pipes to freeze?

Pipes can start to freeze when the air around them reaches about 20 degrees Fahrenheit. Outside temperature is only part of it. Wind exposure, insulation, and how long the cold lasts all change the answer. In an uninsulated exterior wall during a windy 15-degree night, freezing can happen in a few hours.

### Should I leave my faucets dripping all winter?

No. Dripping faucets are a short-term tactic for the coldest nights of the year, not a default for every winter day. Use the drip method when temperatures are forecast to fall below zero or when wind chills are well below zero. The rest of the winter, focus on insulation, air sealing, and keeping the thermostat at 55 or higher.

### Can plastic pipes freeze and burst the same as copper?

Yes. PEX and CPVC pipes are more forgiving than copper because they can flex a little as water freezes, but they can still split if the freeze is severe or repeated. Do not assume a plastic supply line is safe just because it is plastic. The same insulation and air sealing steps apply.

### How much does it cost to fix a burst pipe?

A simple repair on an accessible copper or PEX line typically runs a few hundred dollars. The damage from the water that came out of it almost always costs more. Drywall, flooring, baseboards, and mold remediation often push total repair costs past $10,000, which matches what most insurance carriers see on frozen pipe claims.

### Will homeowners insurance cover a frozen pipe burst?

Usually yes, but only if you took reasonable steps to prevent it. Most policies require you to maintain heat in the home and shut off and drain pipes if the house will be vacant. If your thermostat was off or set far below freezing, a claim can be denied. Check your policy and read the winter clauses before you travel.
