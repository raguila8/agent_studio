**TL;DR:** If your furnace isn't heating, run six quick checks before calling anyone: thermostat (batteries, mode, setpoint), the circuit breaker, the air filter, the blower or service switch on the furnace, the condensate drain on high-efficiency units, and the pilot light or igniter on older ones. Anything past that, especially the gas valve, flame sensor, or sealed combustion chamber, is a licensed pro's job. And if your CO alarm is chirping, treat it as real until proven otherwise.

---

A furnace that won't heat is one of those problems that feels worse than it usually is. You wake up, the house is 58 degrees, and the first thing you do is check your phone for an HVAC company that's open. That's a fine instinct in a real emergency. But in maybe half of furnace "no heat" calls, the actual fix is something a homeowner could have handled in fifteen minutes, with no tools and no risk.

This guide walks through the safe checks first, in the order a good technician would do them. It also draws a clear line at where homeowner troubleshooting stops and pro work begins. Anything involving the gas valve, the flame sensor, sealed combustion, or the inside of the burner assembly is not a DIY job, full stop.

If you're in Springfield or Madison and the checks below don't bring the heat back, we'll come take a look. But try these first.

## Why won't my furnace turn on?

A furnace that won't run is almost always missing one of three things: a "heat" signal from the thermostat, electrical power to the unit itself, or fuel and combustion air to actually fire. Most no-heat calls trace back to a dead thermostat battery, a tripped breaker, a clogged filter, a flipped service switch, or a blocked condensate drain. Real mechanical failures are the minority.

The reason that matters is sequencing. A furnace runs through a startup sequence every time it gets a call for heat, and any one missing piece will stop the whole thing. Working through the simple stuff first is how techs actually diagnose these, and it's why a $200 service call can sometimes end with "your thermostat needs a AA battery."

The checks below follow that same order. Start at the top and work down.

## Is the thermostat actually calling for heat?

The thermostat is the brain of the system, and a surprising number of "broken furnace" calls are really dead thermostats. Before you do anything else, confirm the display is on, the mode is set to "heat" (not "cool" or "off"), and the setpoint is at least a few degrees above the current room temperature. If the screen is blank or flickering, replace the batteries.

A few specific things to check:

- Battery-powered thermostats usually need new batteries every 12 months. A weak battery can leave the display lit but unable to send a heat signal.
- Smart thermostats can lose their Wi-Fi schedule after a power blip and end up in "off" or "eco" mode without you realizing.
- If you have a programmable schedule, confirm it's not in a setback period (some are programmed down to 60 degrees overnight).
- A loose wire on a manually-mounted thermostat will cause intermittent heat. If you have a screwdriver and feel comfortable pulling the faceplate, you can check that the wires are seated. If not, leave it for a tech.

If the thermostat is on, set correctly, and the furnace still won't start, move on.

## Did the furnace's breaker trip?

Yes, your furnace runs on electricity even if it burns gas. The blower motor, control board, igniter, and inducer fan all draw power from a dedicated 120-volt circuit (or 240V for electric furnaces). If that breaker is tripped, the furnace is dead no matter how much gas is at the valve.

Open your electrical panel and find the breaker labeled "furnace" or "HVAC." If the switch is in the middle position or fully off, push it all the way off, then firmly back on. If it trips again right away, stop and call a pro. A breaker that won't hold is telling you something, and we cover the reasons in [why a circuit breaker keeps tripping](/blog/circuit-breaker-keeps-tripping). Repeated resets on a faulty circuit are a documented fire risk per [CPSC electrical safety guidance](https://www.cpsc.gov/Safety-Education/Safety-Education-Centers/Electrical-Safety).

## When is a dirty filter the actual cause?

A clogged air filter is one of the most common reasons a furnace short-cycles, blows cold air, or shuts down entirely. When airflow drops, the heat exchanger overheats, and a safety switch called the high-limit shuts the burners off to prevent damage. From your end, it looks like the furnace fires for a minute and then quits.

The [U.S. Department of Energy](https://www.energy.gov/energysaver/maintaining-your-air-conditioner) recommends checking forced-air filters every month during heavy-use season and replacing them at least every three months. If you can't see light through the filter when you hold it up, it's done. Slide in a fresh one (note the airflow arrow on the frame; it points toward the furnace), and give the system 10 to 15 minutes to reset before testing.

If your filter looked fine and the furnace is still cycling off after a minute or two, you may have a blocked return vent, a closed damper, or a dirty blower wheel. The first two you can check yourself. The last one is a pro's job.

## What is the furnace service switch and where is it?

Right next to the furnace itself, usually mounted on a wall or joist within arm's reach, there's a switch that looks like a regular light switch. That's the service switch (sometimes called the "blower switch" or "furnace disconnect"), and it cuts all power to the unit. It exists so techs can work on the furnace without going to the panel. It's also famously easy to flip off by accident.

Things to check:

- Confirm the switch is in the "on" position. The plate is often plain white and unlabeled.
- In basements and attics, this switch gets bumped during storage, holiday decorating, or kids playing.
- Some furnaces also have a door safety switch that cuts power if the blower compartment cover isn't fully seated. If the cover was off for a filter change, make sure it's snapped back on tight.

If the service switch was off, flip it on, wait a minute, then try the thermostat again.

## Is the condensate drain backed up on a high-efficiency furnace?

If your furnace has white PVC pipes coming out of the side or top (instead of just a metal flue), it's a high-efficiency, condensing unit. These furnaces extract so much heat from combustion that water vapor in the exhaust actually condenses to liquid inside the unit. That water has to drain somewhere. When the drain clogs, a float switch shuts the furnace down to prevent flooding.

Per [Energy Star's guidance on high-efficiency furnaces](https://www.energystar.gov/products/heating_cooling/furnaces), these units (AFUE 90% and above) all rely on a condensate system. Signs the drain is the problem:

- Water pooling near the base of the furnace
- A small reservoir or trap that looks full or has visible debris
- The furnace runs for a few seconds, then shuts off with no error code, or with a code referencing "pressure switch"

You can sometimes clear a slow drain by detaching the PVC trap and flushing it with warm water and a small amount of dish soap or distilled vinegar. If you're not comfortable taking the trap apart, leave it. A tech will clear it in 10 minutes.

## Pilot light or igniter: which one do you actually have?

Furnaces made before roughly 1990 generally have a standing pilot light, a small flame that burns 24/7 and lights the burners on demand. Furnaces from the early 1990s onward use either a hot surface igniter (a small ceramic element that glows orange) or an intermittent spark igniter. The check is different for each.

For a standing pilot:

- Look through the small inspection window near the base of the burner compartment. You should see a steady blue flame.
- If it's out, follow the relighting instructions printed on the furnace's interior label, exactly as written. Most modern standing pilots have a clearly labeled gas valve with "off," "pilot," and "on" positions.
- If the pilot lights but won't stay lit, the thermocouple is likely bad. That's a pro repair.

For a hot surface or spark igniter, do not try to inspect or service it yourself. The ceramic on a hot surface igniter is brittle and a thumbprint can shorten its life. If you hear the furnace try to fire (a click or whirr) but never light, the igniter, flame sensor, or gas valve is the likely culprit, and all three are pro repairs.

## When do you stop troubleshooting and call a pro?

Anything past the simple checks above is licensed-tech territory. The [Air Conditioning Contractors of America](https://www.acca.org/) (ACCA) and most state mechanical codes treat fuel-burning appliance work as restricted for good reason: a small mistake with gas, combustion air, or venting can kill people. Specifically, leave these alone:

- The gas valve, gas line, or any pipe fitting
- The flame sensor, hot surface igniter, or spark electrode
- The heat exchanger or burner assembly (a cracked heat exchanger can leak CO into the house)
- The inducer motor, pressure switch, or sealed combustion chamber
- Any furnace wiring beyond the thermostat low-voltage connections

You also need a pro if you smell gas (leave the house first, then call your utility from outside), if you see soot around the furnace, or if the flame is yellow or orange instead of blue. Combustion appliance safety is governed by [NFPA 54, the National Fuel Gas Code](https://www.nfpa.org/codes-and-standards/all-codes-and-standards/list-of-codes-and-standards/detail?code=54), and the work has to be done by someone qualified under it.

## What about your carbon monoxide alarm?

Carbon monoxide (CO) is the one risk you cannot ignore, and it's the reason "just run it a little longer and see" is a bad strategy with a misbehaving furnace. CO is colorless, odorless, and at high enough concentrations it kills before symptoms get serious. The [CDC reports](https://www.cdc.gov/carbon-monoxide/about/index.html) hundreds of accidental, non-fire CO deaths in the U.S. each year, most from home heating equipment and generators.

Two things every homeowner should know:

- A CO alarm with a "low battery" chirp sounds different from a real CO alarm. A real alarm is loud, continuous, and usually repeats every few seconds. If you're not sure, treat it as real, get everyone outside, and call 911 from outside the house.
- CO alarms have a finite lifespan, typically 7 to 10 years even if the battery is fresh. The [Consumer Product Safety Commission](https://www.cpsc.gov/Newsroom/News-Releases/2023/CPSC-Reminds-Consumers-of-the-Dangers-of-Carbon-Monoxide-and-the-Importance-of-Working-CO-Alarms) recommends replacing the unit itself per the manufacturer's date stamp, not just the batteries.

If your furnace is acting up and your CO alarm goes off, the two are not unrelated until proven otherwise. Don't keep cycling the furnace. Get fresh air, call a pro.

## Frequently asked questions

### Why is my furnace blowing cold air?

Usually one of three things. The thermostat fan is set to "on" instead of "auto," so the blower runs even between heat cycles and pushes room-temperature air. The furnace tried to fire but the burners didn't ignite, so the blower is running on a safety timer. Or the high-limit safety switch tripped because of a dirty filter or blocked airflow. Check the thermostat fan setting first, then the filter.

### How long should it take a furnace to heat the house?

A properly sized furnace should raise indoor temperature roughly 1 to 2 degrees per 15 to 30 minutes in cold weather, depending on insulation, outdoor temperature, and how cold the house got. If your furnace runs constantly and never reaches setpoint, you may have an undersized unit, a duct leak, or a thermostat that's miscalibrated. The [Department of Energy](https://www.energy.gov/energysaver/home-heating-systems) has good background on heating system sizing and efficiency.

### Is it safe to keep running a furnace that keeps shutting off?

No. Repeated short-cycling stresses the heat exchanger and can mask a real problem like a cracked exchanger, a failing flame sensor, or a venting issue. Any of those can let CO into the house. Shut the system off at the thermostat (or the service switch if needed), and either work through the checks above or call a tech.

### My pilot light keeps going out. Can I just keep relighting it?

You can relight it once or twice, but if it won't stay lit, you have a real failure, usually a bad thermocouple or a dirty pilot orifice. Both are pro repairs. The thermocouple is a small metal probe that senses the pilot flame and tells the gas valve it's safe to stay open. When it fails, the gas valve shuts off, which is the right behavior, but means the furnace won't heat.
