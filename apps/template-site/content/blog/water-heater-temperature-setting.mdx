**TL;DR:** Most households should set the water heater to 120°F. That is the temperature the [U.S. Department of Energy recommends](https://www.energy.gov/energysaver/water-heating) for safe, efficient hot water. If anyone in your home has a weakened immune system, bump it to 130–140°F and add a thermostatic mixing valve at the tank or fixtures to prevent scalds. Anything above 140°F is wasted energy and a real burn hazard.

---

Pick up the water heater dial debate online and you will see two camps yelling past each other. One says crank it to 140°F to kill bacteria. The other says drop it to 120°F to save money and protect kids. Both are partly right, and the correct water heater temperature for your home depends on who lives there.

This guide walks through what the federal agencies actually recommend, how fast hot water can burn skin, why Legionella matters, and how to change the setting without making things worse. If your unit is old or the dial has no numbers, we will tell you when to stop and call a plumber.

## What temperature should a home water heater be set to?

For most homes, 120°F is the right setting. The [Department of Energy](https://www.energy.gov/energysaver/water-heating) and [Energy Star](https://www.energystar.gov/products/water_heaters) both recommend 120°F as the default. It delivers comfortable hot water, prevents most scald injuries, and keeps standby heat loss down. Households with high Legionella risk should run 130–140°F with a mixing valve installed.

Water heater manufacturers ship most new tanks pre-set somewhere between 120°F and 140°F. The factory default is not always the right number for your house, so it is worth checking with a meat or candy thermometer at the closest hot water tap. Let the water run for a full minute before measuring.

The 120°F number is not arbitrary. It is the point where the math on energy savings, scald prevention, and bacteria control balances out for an average household. Once you understand what changes at higher and lower settings, you can make a smart call for your own home.

## Why does the DOE recommend 120°F?

The Department of Energy recommends 120°F because it cuts standby heat loss and the risk of severe burns without leaving you without hot water. The [DOE estimates](https://www.energy.gov/energysaver/water-heating) you can save 4–22% on water heating costs by lowering the tank from 140°F to 120°F, depending on usage and household size.

Standby heat loss is the energy your tank burns just to keep stored water hot when no one is using it. The hotter the water, the bigger the gap with the surrounding air, and the faster heat leaks out through the tank walls. Over a year, that adds up.

Water heating is the [second-largest energy user in a typical home](https://www.energy.gov/energysaver/water-heating), behind only space heating and cooling. For an electric tank running 24/7, every 10°F you can shave off the thermostat shows up on the bill. The savings are smaller for gas, but still real.

## How dangerous is 140°F water on bare skin?

Very dangerous, especially for children and older adults. At 140°F, water can cause a third-degree burn in as little as [5 seconds of contact](https://www.cdc.gov/safechild/burns/index.html). At 120°F, the same burn takes about 5 minutes. That is why pediatricians and burn surgeons both push the lower setting for most homes.

The [American Academy of Pediatrics](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/safety-prevention/at-home/Pages/Burn-Prevention.aspx) recommends 120°F as the maximum water heater setting for homes with children. Kids have thinner skin than adults and pull their hand back slower, so they get a deeper burn from the same water in the same time.

Older adults are in the same category. Slower reaction times and thinner skin mean a tap that an adult shrugs off can send a grandparent to the emergency room. [Tap water burns](https://www.ameriburn.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/04/burnpreventionforolderadults.pdf) account for thousands of hospital visits a year in the U.S., and most are preventable with a thermostat adjustment.

## Doesn't 140°F kill Legionella bacteria?

Yes, but it is more complicated than flipping the dial. Legionella, the bacteria that causes Legionnaires' disease, grows best between [77°F and 113°F](https://www.cdc.gov/legionella/wmp/overview/growth-and-spread.html) and dies quickly above 140°F. The [CDC and OSHA](https://www.osha.gov/legionnaires-disease/control-prevention) recommend storing water above 140°F in buildings that serve high-risk people, with delivery temperatures controlled by a mixing valve.

In a healthy household, the practical risk from a 120°F tank is low. Legionella becomes a real concern when someone in the home is immunocompromised, has chronic lung disease, is over 50 and smokes, or has had a recent organ transplant. The [CDC's risk groups](https://www.cdc.gov/legionella/about/causes-transmission.html) are worth a quick read if you are unsure.

The compromise that hospitals and assisted living facilities use is simple: store hot, deliver warm. The tank runs at 140°F or higher to suppress bacteria. A thermostatic mixing valve, sometimes called an ASSE 1017 valve, mixes cold water in before the hot water reaches your faucets, so what comes out of the tap is a safe 120°F.

The [American Water Works Association](https://www.awwa.org/) and major plumbing codes both recognize this approach. It costs more upfront, but it is the only way to get both safety benefits at once.

## How do you find the right setting for your household?

Match the setting to who lives in your home. A simple rule of thumb:

- Healthy adults only: 120°F.
- Young children in the home: 120°F, never higher.
- Older adults with normal health: 120°F.
- Anyone immunocompromised, on chemotherapy, with chronic lung disease, or recovering from a transplant: 130–140°F with a mixing valve set to deliver 120°F at the tap.
- Mixed household (kids and an immunocompromised grandparent): 140°F at the tank, mixing valve at every fixture kids touch.

If you are not sure which bucket you fall into, talk to your doctor. The Legionella risk is not theoretical for some people, and the burn risk is not theoretical for kids. A plumber can install the mixing valve, but the medical call is yours.

For a typical family with no high-risk members, 120°F is the right answer. You get the energy savings, the scald protection, and a tiny Legionella risk that is well below the level public health agencies worry about for residential settings.

## How to change your water heater temperature safely

Most thermostats can be adjusted in under five minutes, but the steps depend on whether you have a gas or electric tank. Always turn the power or gas off before opening any access panel. If your unit is more than 10 years old, the dial is unmarked, or you smell gas, [call a licensed plumber](/contact) instead of guessing.

For a **gas water heater**, the thermostat dial is usually on the front of the tank, near the bottom, next to the gas control valve. Turn the dial down or up in small increments. The markings are often vague (warm, hot, very hot), so measure the tap water 4–6 hours later with a thermometer and adjust again if needed.

For an **electric water heater**, there are usually two thermostats, one upper and one lower, hidden behind access panels. Shut off the breaker at the panel first. Remove the access cover, peel back the insulation, and use a flathead screwdriver to turn the dial. Set both thermostats to the same number. Replace the insulation and cover before turning the breaker back on.

After any change, run the closest hot water tap for a full minute and measure the temperature. Tanks take 3–6 hours to fully restabilize, so re-check the next morning. If the temperature is far off from where you set the dial, the thermostat or heating element may be failing, which is a job for a [plumbing and HVAC technician](/services/plumbing-hvac).

## When should you call a plumber instead?

Call a plumber if the dial is unmarked, the tank is over 10 years old, the temperature is not responding to thermostat changes, you smell gas, or you see corrosion or rust around the tank. Older tanks can have failed thermostats, stuck dip tubes, or sediment buildup that masks the real temperature. Guessing in those cases can leave you with scalding water at one tap and cold water at another.

A pro can also test the tank's recovery rate, flush sediment, check the anode rod, and install a mixing valve if your household needs both Legionella protection and scald protection. None of that is DIY work for most homeowners, and the parts cost is small compared to a tank replacement caused by neglected maintenance.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is 120°F hot enough for a dishwasher?

Yes. Modern dishwashers have internal heating elements that boost incoming water to the right temperature for the cycle. [Energy Star certified dishwashers](https://www.energystar.gov/products/dishwashers) are designed to work with 120°F inlet water. If your dishes are coming out greasy, the issue is usually detergent, loading, or a clogged spray arm, not the water heater.

### How much can I save by lowering my water heater from 140°F to 120°F?

The Department of Energy estimates [4–22% off your water heating bill](https://www.energy.gov/energysaver/water-heating), depending on household size and how much hot water you use. For an average family running an electric tank, that is roughly $30–$60 a year. Bigger savings come from also insulating the tank and the first few feet of hot water pipe.

### How long does it take a tank to reach a new temperature setting?

Plan on 3 to 6 hours for a full tank to fully stabilize at the new setting. A 40-gallon gas tank reheats faster than a 50-gallon electric one. Take your first measurement after at least 3 hours, and a second the next morning, before making another adjustment.

### Can I set my tankless water heater the same way?

Tankless units make this easier because the temperature is set digitally and the unit only heats water on demand, so standby loss is not a factor. The same scald and Legionella guidance applies. Set the unit to 120°F for healthy households, higher with a mixing valve for high-risk households.

### Does a hotter tank give me more hot water?

A little, but not the way most people think. Raising the tank temperature lets a mixing valve at the fixtures dilute hot water with cold, stretching your effective hot water supply. Without a mixing valve, simply turning the dial up just gives you the same volume of water at a more dangerous temperature. If you are constantly running out of hot water, the real fix is a larger tank, a tankless unit, or a recirculation system, not a hotter setting.
