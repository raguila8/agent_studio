If the last contractor you hired left you with a half-finished job or a bill that doubled, you already know why this matters. A contractor estimate is your first real look at how someone runs their business, and most of the damage in a bad project shows up here, before any tools come out. Knowing what to expect from a contractor estimate turns a confusing sales visit into a clear comparison you can actually act on.

This guide walks through the whole process in plain English: the phone call, the on-site visit, what a real written quote looks like, what "free estimate" actually covers, the difference between an estimate and a binding bid, and the warning signs that should send you back to your shortlist. It's written for homeowners who've been burned before and want to make sure it doesn't happen again.

## What is a contractor estimate, exactly?

A contractor estimate is a written or verbal price range for a specific scope of work, based on a site visit and a conversation about what you want done. It is not a binding contract. It's the contractor's best read on cost, materials, and timeline given what they can see today, and it's the document you use to compare bids before hiring.

A good estimate is detailed enough that another contractor could look at it and understand the job. A vague one-line number on the back of a business card is not an estimate. It's a guess. The [Federal Trade Commission's guidance on hiring a contractor](https://consumer.ftc.gov/articles/hiring-contractor) recommends getting written estimates from at least three contractors and comparing them line by line before you commit.

## What happens during the phone intake?

The phone intake is a five to fifteen minute call where the contractor (or their office) asks what you need, when you need it, and whether the job is something they actually do. Expect questions about the address, the age of the home, the symptoms you're seeing, and your timeline. A good intake also confirms whether the visit is free.

This is also your first read on how the company operates. Did someone answer? Did they call back the same day? Did they ask intelligent questions or just push for a sales appointment? The [Better Business Bureau's advice on home improvement contractors](https://www.bbb.org/all/spot-a-scam/home-improvement-scams) is worth a quick read before you book anything, especially the section on verifying the business has a real local address.

Have these ready before you call:

- A short description of the problem or project
- The age of the system, roof, or area involved
- Photos if it's something visible
- Your rough budget range (it saves everyone time)
- Two or three windows when you can be home

## What happens during the on-site walkthrough?

The on-site walkthrough is when the contractor comes out, looks at the actual work area, takes measurements and photos, and asks follow-up questions. For a small repair it might take twenty minutes. For a roof replacement, panel upgrade, or HVAC install, plan on an hour or more. They should walk the space with you, not just glance from the driveway.

Expect them to look beyond the obvious. A plumber checking a slow drain should also look at the vent stack. A roofer should look in the attic, not just at the shingles. If someone gives you a price before they've actually inspected the work area, that's not an estimate, it's a sales pitch. For a sense of what a thorough inspection looks like, our [roof storm damage inspection checklist](/blog/roof-storm-damage-checklist) shows the kind of detail to expect.

Good estimators also ask about access, parking, pets, and working hours. Those little details get missed in a rushed visit, and missed details become change orders later.

## What should a written contractor quote include?

A real written quote should include the scope of work, materials list with brands and grades, labor, permit costs, exclusions, a timeline with start and finish targets, a payment schedule, the contractor's license and insurance information, and a clear warranty. If any of those are missing, ask for them in writing before you sign.

The [National Association of the Remodeling Industry](https://www.nari.org/) publishes guidance for homeowners on what a complete proposal looks like, and the list above is roughly the floor. Here's what each line actually means:

- **Scope of work.** Written so a stranger could read it and know what's being done. "Replace water heater" is not a scope. "Remove 40-gallon gas water heater, install new 50-gallon high-recovery unit, replace supply lines, update venting to current code" is.
- **Materials.** Brand, model, size, and grade. Generic "shingles" or "panel" means the contractor can swap in whatever's cheapest on install day.
- **Labor.** A flat number per task or hours times rate. A single project price is fine if the scope is locked.
- **Permit costs.** Whether permits are included, who pulls them, and who pays the fees. Per the [International Code Council](https://www.iccsafe.org/), most structural, electrical, plumbing, mechanical, and reroofing work requires a permit. A contractor who suggests skipping it is a hard no.
- **Exclusions.** What's not in the price: disposal fees, drywall repair after access cuts, paint touch-ups, code upgrades discovered during work. This list is where surprise bills hide.
- **Timeline.** Start date window, working days estimate, and a finish target. Weather and material lead times can move dates, and good contractors say so.
- **Payment schedule.** Tied to milestones, not the calendar.
- **License and insurance.** State or local license number, general liability, and workers' comp. You should be able to verify these in five minutes.
- **Warranty.** Labor warranty length and what it covers, plus any manufacturer warranties on materials.

## What does "free estimate" actually mean?

"Free estimate" usually means the contractor won't charge you for a basic site visit and a written quote on a defined job. It does not always cover diagnostic work, engineering, custom design, or load calculations. Specialty trades sometimes charge a service fee that gets credited toward the work if you hire them.

Ask up front. A direct question like "is this visit free, and is there anything you'd charge for separately?" gets you a clean answer. For complex projects (whole-home rewires, multi-zone HVAC redesigns, structural changes), a small design fee is normal and often a sign of a serious contractor. According to the [National Association of Home Builders](https://www.nahb.org/), more involved remodels typically include a paid design or planning phase before construction pricing is finalized.

## Estimate vs. bid vs. fixed-price contract

These three words get used loosely, but they mean different things, and the difference matters when something changes mid-job.

- **Estimate.** A best-guess price range based on visible conditions. Not binding. Useful for comparing contractors and budgeting.
- **Bid.** A firm price offered to do a defined scope of work. Once you accept it, it usually becomes the contract price.
- **Fixed-price (or lump-sum) contract.** A binding agreement where the contractor takes on the cost risk for the agreed scope. Changes happen through written change orders.

[Consumer Reports' coverage of contractor hiring](https://www.consumerreports.org/home-improvement/) notes that fixed-price contracts protect homeowners on well-defined jobs, while time-and-materials makes more sense when the scope genuinely can't be pinned down (think hidden water damage behind a wall). Either way, the contract should say which model you're working under and how change orders get priced and approved.

## Red flags that should stop the conversation

Some warning signs are minor. A few are serious enough that the right move is to thank the contractor for their time and call someone else. Trust your gut on the small stuff and walk on any of these:

- **Door-to-door sales after a storm.** Storm chasers follow weather. The [Insurance Information Institute's guidance on contractor fraud](https://www.iii.org/) and warnings from state attorney general offices both flag this pattern every year. Reputable local contractors don't need to knock on doors.
- **Large cash deposits up front.** Many states cap deposits at 10% or $1,000, whichever is less. Anything over a third of the job before work starts is a red flag.
- **No written license or insurance.** A real contractor can produce their license number and a certificate of insurance the same day. If they "left it in the truck" twice, they don't have it.
- **Verbal-only pricing.** If they won't put it in writing, there is nothing to enforce later.
- **Pressure to sign today.** "This price is only good if you sign now" is a sales tactic, not a real discount. Real estimates hold for at least a week or two.
- **No physical business address.** A PO box and a cell number is not a business. The [BBB's home improvement scam guidance](https://www.bbb.org/all/spot-a-scam/home-improvement-scams) and [Angi's research on contractor disputes](https://www.angi.com/research/) both repeat this one for a reason.
- **Asking you to pull the permit.** If a contractor wants the homeowner to pull the permit on work the contractor is doing, they're shifting liability. Don't.
- **Cash-only or wire-only payments.** Pay by check or card so there's a paper trail.

If you smell something off, you usually aren't wrong. Get a second quote.

## Questions worth asking before you sign

You don't need a legal degree to ask smart questions during an estimate. A short list, asked plainly, tells you more than an hour of small talk:

- How long have you been doing this kind of work in this area?
- Can I see your license number and a current certificate of insurance?
- Who's actually on site every day, you or a crew?
- Are permits included, and who pulls them?
- What's your payment schedule, and is the deposit refundable?
- What's not included in this price?
- What does your warranty cover, and for how long?
- Can I talk to two recent customers with similar jobs?

A contractor who answers these calmly and in plain language is usually the one you want. Stalling, deflecting, or getting defensive on basic questions is its own answer.

## What to do after you have the quotes

Lay your two or three written quotes next to each other and compare line by line, not just totals. The cheapest number is often missing scope, not winning on efficiency. Ask each contractor to explain anything that doesn't match.

## Frequently asked questions

### How many contractor estimates should I get?

Three is the standard recommendation, and it's what most consumer protection groups, including the [FTC's hiring guidance](https://consumer.ftc.gov/articles/hiring-contractor), suggest. Two is fine if the second quote is clearly better in every way. One is never enough, even if the first contractor was referred by someone you trust.

### Is it normal for a contractor to charge for an estimate?

For simple, well-defined jobs (a water heater swap, a repair call, a single-room reroof), most contractors offer free estimates. For complex projects that require design, load calculations, or extensive measurement, a paid assessment is common. According to the [NAHB](https://www.nahb.org/), paid pre-construction services are standard on larger remodels and often credited back if you hire them.

### How long is a contractor estimate good for?

Most written estimates are valid for 30 to 60 days. After that, material prices and crew availability can shift enough that the number isn't reliable. The estimate document should say the expiration date on it. If it doesn't, ask, and get the answer in writing.

### How much deposit should I pay a contractor?

A reasonable deposit is usually 10% to 30% of the contract price, with the rest tied to milestones like rough-in, inspection, and completion. Some states cap deposits by law. [Consumer Reports' contractor guidance](https://www.consumerreports.org/home-improvement/) and most state attorney general offices flag any request for more than a third of the job up front as a warning sign.

### What if the actual work costs more than the estimate?

Legitimate cost increases happen, especially when hidden conditions (rotted framing, old wiring, undersized supply lines) show up after demo. They should always come to you as a written change order before the work happens, with the new price and a reason. If a contractor adds charges to the final bill without a signed change order, you generally don't have to pay them. Check your contract and, if needed, your state's home improvement laws.
