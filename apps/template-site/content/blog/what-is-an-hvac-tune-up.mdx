So you got a postcard in the mail offering a $79 HVAC tune-up, and now you are wondering whether it actually does anything or whether the technician is just there to sell you a new system. Fair question. The honest answer is that a real HVAC tune-up is one of the cheapest pieces of home maintenance you can buy, but the industry is full of "tune-ups" that are really just a 20-minute walkthrough and a sales pitch. This guide walks through what should actually be on the checklist, what the work is worth, and how to tell the two apart before you write a check.

## What is an HVAC tune-up, exactly?

An HVAC tune-up is a yearly preventive maintenance visit where a licensed technician inspects, tests, cleans, and recalibrates the parts of your heating or cooling system that wear out, drift out of spec, or get dirty over time. It is not a repair visit. The goal is to catch small problems before they become breakdowns.

The work itself is straightforward, but it has to be done thoroughly. [Energy Star's maintenance checklist](https://www.energystar.gov/saveathome/heating_cooling/maintenance) lists the same core tasks the industry has used for decades: clean the coils, check the refrigerant, tighten the electrical connections, lubricate the motor, inspect the drain, and verify the thermostat is reading right. The trade association version, [ACCA Standard 4 (Quality Maintenance of HVAC Systems)](https://www.acca.org/standards/approved-standards), goes further and spells out the exact procedures a professional tune-up should follow.

If your shop cannot tell you what is on their checklist before the appointment, that is the first sign you are buying a sales call, not maintenance.

## What does a real HVAC tune-up include?

A proper tune-up covers seven core areas: coil cleaning, refrigerant charge, electrical contacts and wiring, the capacitor, blower motor amp draw, the condensate drain line, and thermostat calibration. The technician should test each one, document the readings, and explain anything that is out of spec. The whole visit usually runs 60 to 90 minutes per system.

Here is what each piece actually means in plain English.

### Coil cleaning

Your system has two coils, one inside and one outside. They are the parts that move heat in or out of your house. When they get coated in dust, pollen, or grass clippings, the system has to work harder to move the same amount of air. The [U.S. Department of Energy notes that a dirty condenser coil can raise energy use by 30 percent](https://www.energy.gov/energysaver/maintaining-your-air-conditioner) and shorten the life of the compressor. A real tune-up includes rinsing the outdoor coil and inspecting the indoor coil for buildup.

### Refrigerant charge check

Too little refrigerant means the system cannot cool properly. Too much can damage the compressor. A technician should hook up gauges, read the pressures, and compare them to the manufacturer's spec for your equipment and the outdoor temperature. If the charge is off, that almost always means there is a leak that needs to be found, not just "topped off."

### Electrical contacts and wiring

Loose or corroded electrical connections are one of the most common causes of HVAC failures. The technician should kill power at the disconnect, inspect every connection at the contactor and control board, and tighten anything that has worked loose. Burned or pitted contacts get replaced before they fail.

### Capacitor test

The capacitor is a small cylindrical part that gives the compressor and fan motors the jolt they need to start. Capacitors degrade over time, and a weak one will make your AC short-cycle and eventually kill the compressor. A real tune-up measures the capacitor's microfarad rating with a meter and compares it to the rating printed on the side. Out-of-spec gets replaced.

### Blower motor amp draw

The technician should clamp a meter on the blower motor and verify it is pulling the amperage the manufacturer expects. High amp draw usually means a dirty blower wheel or a failing motor. Catching this early is the difference between a $30 cleaning and a $700 motor replacement.

### Condensate drain line

Your AC pulls humidity out of the air, and that water has to drain somewhere. Drain lines clog with algae and biofilm every year. When they back up, the safety switch shuts the system off, or worse, water spills into your ceiling. A tune-up should include flushing the drain and treating it with a tablet or vinegar.

### Thermostat calibration

The technician should verify the thermostat is reading the actual room temperature, that the heat anticipator (on older units) is set right, and that the system cycles on and off when it should. A miscalibrated thermostat can make the system run all day chasing a setpoint it will never hit.

## How much does an HVAC tune-up cost?

Most reputable shops charge $80 to $200 per system for a standalone tune-up, with regional variation. Maintenance plans that cover two visits a year (one for AC, one for the furnace) usually run $150 to $300 annually. Beware of $39 or $49 promo tune-ups; they almost always exist to get a salesperson in your door.

Pricing comes down to two things: how long the visit takes and what is included. A 90-minute visit with coil cleaning, refrigerant readings, capacitor testing, and a written report costs the shop real money in labor and supplies. A $49 "tune-up" that takes 20 minutes is loss-leader marketing, and you should expect the technician to find an expensive problem on the way out.

If you have a maintenance plan with a contractor you trust, that is usually the better deal. Most plans include priority scheduling, a discount on repairs, and waived diagnostic fees. ## Is an HVAC tune-up actually worth the money?

For most homeowners with equipment more than two years old, yes. A real tune-up usually pays for itself in the same year through lower energy bills and prevented repairs, and it extends the system's useful life by several years. Skipping maintenance is the single most common reason an HVAC system fails 5 to 7 years before it should.

The hard numbers are well-documented. [Energy Star estimates that a neglected system loses about 5 percent of its efficiency every year](https://www.energystar.gov/saveathome/heating_cooling/maintenance), which compounds quickly. A 16 SEER AC that has gone three years without maintenance can be operating closer to 13 SEER in real-world performance. On a typical Springfield or Madison summer, that is the difference between a $180 power bill and a $230 one, every month.

Equipment life is the bigger factor. Central AC units are designed to last 15 to 20 years, and furnaces 15 to 25, according to [ASHRAE's equipment service life data](https://www.ashrae.org/technical-resources/bookstore/equipment-life-expectancy). The ones that hit those numbers are almost always the ones that got serviced every year. The ones that die at year 8 or 10 are almost always the ones that did not.

The other piece is warranty coverage. Most major manufacturers (Carrier, Trane, Lennox, Rheem) require documented annual maintenance to honor the 10-year parts warranty. If your compressor fails in year 7 and you have no maintenance records, you may be paying for the replacement out of pocket.

## When should you schedule a tune-up?

Schedule your AC tune-up in early spring (March or April) and your furnace tune-up in early fall (September or October). Booking before the seasonal rush gets you better appointment windows, less expensive labor at some shops, and a system that is ready to run before the first heat wave or cold snap.

The reason matters. If your AC has been sitting through a Midwest winter, it has not run for five or six months. Spiders, leaves, and dust have settled in places they should not be. A spring tune-up clears all of that and verifies the system will actually start when you flip it on in May.

Furnaces have an additional safety angle. A fall tune-up should include a combustion analysis and a heat exchanger inspection, since cracked heat exchangers can leak carbon monoxide. The [CDC links several hundred carbon monoxide deaths each year to home heating equipment](https://www.cdc.gov/co/faqs.html), and a yearly inspection is the cheapest way to catch a problem before it becomes one.

## How do you tell a real tune-up from an upsell pitch?

A legitimate tune-up technician arrives with a written checklist, spends 60 to 90 minutes on the equipment, gives you a printed or digital report with readings, and only recommends repairs that are tied to specific measurements. A pitch artist spends 20 minutes, takes a few photos, and tells you the system is on its last legs.

Some specific red flags to watch for:

- The technician spends more time at your kitchen table than at the equipment.
- "Findings" are not backed by readings (capacitor microfarads, refrigerant pressures, amp draws). If they say the capacitor is bad, they should be able to show you the meter reading and the rating printed on the part.
- They recommend a full system replacement on a unit that is less than 10 years old, without showing you what specifically failed.
- They use scare language ("this could catch fire any minute") without documenting the problem.
- They want a same-day decision and offer a discount only if you sign tonight.

A real shop will give you the report, point out what is borderline, recommend the work, and walk out. If the issue is real, it will still be real next week when you have had time to think.

## Frequently asked questions

### How often should I get an HVAC tune-up?

Once a year per system, so two visits annually if you have separate AC and heating. AC gets serviced in spring, the furnace in fall. Heat pumps, which run year-round, can be done in either season, though many techs recommend twice yearly because the equipment works harder than a furnace-AC split system.

### Can I do an HVAC tune-up myself?

You can do part of it. Changing the filter monthly, keeping the outdoor unit clear of leaves and grass, and rinsing the condenser fins with a garden hose are all DIY. Refrigerant readings, electrical work, capacitor testing, and combustion analysis on a furnace need an EPA Section 608 certified technician. The [EPA requires certification](https://www.epa.gov/section608) to handle refrigerant legally.

### Will an HVAC tune-up lower my energy bill?

Usually, yes, especially if the system has gone more than a year without service. Dirty coils, low refrigerant, and a weak capacitor all force the system to run longer to hit the same setpoint. Energy Star puts the typical efficiency loss at around 5 percent per neglected year, so the savings show up fastest on systems that have not been touched in a while.

### Is a $49 tune-up a scam?

Not always, but the math rarely works out. A real tune-up takes 60 to 90 minutes of a licensed technician's time, plus supplies. Shops that charge $49 are using the visit as a lead generator and counting on the technician to "find" expensive repairs. If you go with one, get the findings in writing and get a second opinion before approving anything over $300.

### What is the difference between a tune-up and a service call?

A tune-up is preventive: the system is running fine and you want to keep it that way. A service call is reactive: something is wrong and you need it fixed. Tune-ups are scheduled in advance for a flat rate. Service calls usually include a diagnostic fee plus parts and labor. If you have a maintenance plan, the diagnostic fee is often waived.
