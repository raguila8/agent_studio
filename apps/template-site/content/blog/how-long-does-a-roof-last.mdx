**TL;DR:** How long a roof lasts depends mostly on the material. Asphalt shingles last 15 to 30 years, metal lasts 40 to 70, tile lasts 50 or more, and slate can last a century. Midwest weather (hail, freeze-thaw cycles, ice dams, humid summers) tends to cut those numbers by 20 to 30 percent. Warranty length is not the same as real-world lifespan.

---

If you have ever stood in your driveway squinting up at your shingles, wondering how much life is left in them, you are not alone. "How long does a roof last?" is one of the most common questions homeowners in Springfield and Madison ask us, and the honest answer is, "it depends." A roof's lifespan is shaped by the material, how well it was installed, how steep the slope is, how well it is ventilated, and the weather it has lived through. This guide walks through realistic lifespans by material, how Midwest weather changes the math, the signs your roof is near the end, and how to decide between repair and replacement.

## How long does a roof last on average?

Most residential roofs last between 15 and 70 years, with asphalt shingles falling on the short end and metal, tile, and slate on the long end. The biggest factors are material, install quality, ventilation, and climate exposure. Real-world lifespan is usually shorter than the warranty period.

According to the [National Roofing Contractors Association](https://www.nrca.net/), a roof's true service life depends as much on installation and maintenance as it does on the material itself. A well-installed architectural shingle roof in a mild climate can outlast a poorly installed metal roof in a harsh one. So when someone asks for an average, the honest number is somewhere around 20 to 25 years for the most common roof in America (asphalt shingle), and 40-plus years for everything else.

## Roof lifespan by material

Here is what to expect from each common roofing material, assuming a quality install and average climate. We will get to how the Midwest changes these numbers in the next section.

### 3-tab asphalt shingles: 15 to 20 years

These are the thin, flat, three-cutout shingles you see on a lot of older homes and budget builds. They are the cheapest option up front and the shortest-lived. The [Asphalt Roofing Manufacturers Association](https://www.asphaltroofing.org/) notes that 3-tab shingles are more vulnerable to wind uplift than thicker laminated shingles, which matters a lot in Midwest storm country.

### Architectural (laminated) asphalt shingles: 25 to 30 years

These are the thicker, dimensional shingles that look like wood shake from the street. They cost a little more than 3-tab but last longer, hold up better in wind, and now make up the majority of new asphalt installs. Most come with a 30-year or "lifetime" manufacturer warranty, though as we will cover below, warranty does not equal lifespan.

### Standing seam metal: 40 to 70 years

Metal roofing has come a long way. The [Metal Roofing Alliance](https://www.metalroofing.com/) reports that a properly installed metal roof can last 40 to 70 years, often outliving the house it is on. Standing seam (the vertical-panel style with concealed fasteners) is the most durable residential metal type. It sheds snow well, which matters in Wisconsin and Illinois winters.

### Clay and concrete tile: 50-plus years

Tile is rare in the Midwest because of freeze-thaw concerns, but it does show up. Properly installed and maintained tile can last 50 to 100 years. The catch: individual tiles crack from hail or foot traffic, and the underlayment beneath them usually needs replacement every 20 to 30 years even if the tiles are fine.

### Natural slate: 75 to 100 years

Slate is the longest-lived residential roofing material. Real (not synthetic) slate roofs on historic homes routinely last a century or more. They are heavy, expensive, and require specialized installers, which is why you mostly see them on older estates and churches rather than new builds.

### Wood shake and shingle: 25 to 30 years

Cedar shake and shingle roofs have a warm look but need more upkeep than asphalt. They are also restricted by some local fire codes. In humid Midwest summers, wood roofs can grow moss and algae fast, which shortens lifespan if you do not clean and treat them.

### Flat roof membranes (EPDM, TPO, PVC): 20 to 30 years

If you have a flat or low-slope roof over a porch, addition, or garage, it is probably one of these single-ply membranes. EPDM (the black rubber stuff) lasts 20 to 25 years on average. TPO and PVC (the white membranes) typically last 20 to 30. Seams and flashings are usually what fail first, not the membrane itself.

## How does Midwest weather shorten a roof's lifespan?

Hail, freeze-thaw cycles, ice dams, and high-humidity summers can cut a roof's real-world lifespan by 20 to 30 percent compared with manufacturer estimates. Asphalt shingles take the biggest hit, while metal and slate hold up better. Severe single events (a major hailstorm, an ice dam that backs water into the deck) can end a roof's life in one season.

The Midwest sits in the country's most active severe weather corridor. The [Insurance Institute for Business and Home Safety (IBHS)](https://ibhs.org/) has documented how repeated hail impacts crack the asphalt surface of shingles and knock off the granules that protect them from UV. Once granules are gone, the shingle ages fast. [NOAA's Storm Prediction Center](https://www.spc.noaa.gov/climo/) tracks hail and severe wind events across Illinois and Wisconsin, and both states sit firmly inside the high-frequency zone.

Freeze-thaw cycles are the second silent killer. Water seeps into tiny cracks, freezes, expands, and pries things apart, every single winter. Ice dams (ridges of ice at the eaves that back meltwater under the shingles) are the third. A poorly insulated attic plus a cold roof equals ice dams, and ice dams equal leaks. By the time you notice the ceiling stain, the deck below the shingles may already be rotting.

## What are the signs your roof is near the end of its life?

Curling or cupping shingles, bald spots where granules are missing, dark streaks, sagging rooflines, daylight visible from the attic, and granules collecting in gutters are all signs your roof is near the end. One sign alone is not a verdict. Several together usually means it is time to plan a replacement.

A few specifics to look for from the ground or attic:

- **Curling, cupping, or clawing shingles.** The edges lift or the centers rise. This is the asphalt drying out and shrinking.
- **Bare spots and granule loss.** Look for shiny black patches on the shingles and pile-ups of granules in your gutters or at the downspout splash blocks.
- **Dark streaks or moss.** Streaks are usually algae, which is mostly cosmetic. Moss holds moisture against the shingles and accelerates wear.
- **Sagging or dips in the roofline.** Sight along the ridge from across the street. Any dip or wave usually means deck or rafter damage from long-term moisture.
- **Daylight in the attic.** If you can see light through the roof deck during the day, water can get in too.
- **Repeat leaks.** One leak after a hailstorm is one event. Two or three different leaks in two years usually means the system is done.

Spotting these early matters. Our [how to spot a roof leak early](/blog/how-to-spot-a-roof-leak-early) post covers the indoor signs most homeowners miss until the drywall is already stained.

## Repair or replace? How to decide

The rough rule: if repairs would cost more than 30 percent of replacement and the roof is past two-thirds of its expected lifespan, replace it. Below that, repair. Insurance coverage, planned years in the home, and whether damage is localized or widespread all change the math.

Here is the decision framework we walk homeowners through:

1. **Age vs. expected lifespan.** A 12-year-old architectural shingle roof has plenty of life left. A 24-year-old one is on borrowed time.
2. **Damage scope.** Is it one section after a single storm, or widespread wear across the whole roof? Localized damage repairs well. Widespread wear does not.
3. **Deck condition.** If the plywood or OSB underneath is wet, soft, or rotted, you are not buying a few more years with a patch. The deck has to come off.
4. **Cost ratio.** Once a single repair quote hits 30 to 40 percent of replacement cost, replacement is usually the better long-term spend.
5. **How long you will stay.** If you are selling in two years, a clean repair plus disclosure may make more sense than a full tear-off.
6. **Insurance coverage.** After a hail or wind event, your homeowners policy may cover all or part of replacement. Get the claim filed before any repair work that could complicate the adjuster's inspection.

If you want a second opinion before deciding, a reputable roofer should walk the roof, photograph it, and give you a written assessment without pressuring you into an immediate decision.

## Why warranty length is not the same as real lifespan

A "50-year shingle" or "lifetime warranty" sounds reassuring, but warranty terms cover manufacturing defects, not weather, install errors, or normal wear. Most asphalt shingle warranties pro-rate sharply after the first 10 to 15 years, meaning the payout shrinks every year. The roof's real-world lifespan is usually shorter than the warranty number on the box.

Read the fine print before you anchor your expectations to the marketing number. Common warranty exclusions include:

- Damage from hail, wind, or ice (the things most likely to actually end a Midwest roof)
- Improper ventilation in the attic (which the manufacturer will check before paying a claim)
- Install errors by an uncertified contractor
- Walking on the roof, satellite dishes, and other "foot traffic"
- Algae or moss growth (often a separate, shorter warranty)

The labor portion of a warranty is also separate from the material portion, and is usually much shorter (one to two years for most contractors, longer for certified-installer programs). Ask both questions before you sign: how long does the material warranty pay out at 100 percent, and how long does the workmanship warranty cover labor?

## How can you make your roof last longer?

You can add 3 to 5 years of useful life to most roofs by keeping gutters clear, trimming overhanging branches, ensuring proper attic ventilation, removing debris and moss promptly, and catching small leaks before they spread. Annual inspections and prompt minor repairs are the biggest levers.

A few specific habits that pay off:

- **Clean gutters twice a year.** Clogged gutters back water up under the shingles at the eaves, which is exactly where ice dams form too.
- **Trim back branches.** Branches scrape granules off shingles in the wind and drop wet leaves that hold moisture.
- **Check attic ventilation.** Hot, humid attic air bakes shingles from below and shortens lifespan. Soffit and ridge vents should be unblocked.
- **Get an inspection after big storms.** After a hail event over 1 inch or a wind event over 60 mph, have someone walk the roof.
- **Stay on top of small repairs.** A single popped nail or lifted flashing fixed in spring is cheaper than the water damage it would cause by fall.

A roof on a [maintenance plan](/services/maintenance-plans) tends to last noticeably longer than one that never gets looked at until something goes wrong.

## Frequently asked questions

### How long does a typical asphalt shingle roof last in the Midwest?

In Springfield, Madison, and similar Midwest climates, expect 18 to 22 years from an architectural asphalt shingle roof and 12 to 17 years from a 3-tab. Hail, freeze-thaw, and ice dams tend to shorten the manufacturer's stated lifespan by about 20 to 30 percent.

### Can a roof last 50 years?

Yes, but usually only metal, tile, or slate. A standing seam metal roof can last 40 to 70 years, clay or concrete tile can last 50 to 100, and slate can last 75 to 100 or more, per the [Metal Roofing Alliance](https://www.metalroofing.com/) and NRCA data. Asphalt shingles realistically max out around 30, even when the box says 50.

### Does a metal roof really last twice as long as shingles?

In most cases, yes. A properly installed standing seam metal roof typically lasts two to three times as long as architectural asphalt shingles. It costs more up front, but the per-year cost is often lower, especially in hail-prone areas where shingle replacements happen on an insurance cycle.

### How do I know if I need a full replacement or just repairs?

A few patched leaks or one missing shingle is a repair. Widespread granule loss, multiple unrelated leaks, sagging, or a roof past two-thirds of its expected age usually means replacement. If a single repair quote hits 30 to 40 percent of replacement cost, you are spending good money on a roof that is already done.

### Will my homeowners insurance pay for a new roof?

Sometimes. Most policies cover sudden damage (hail, wind, falling tree) but not gradual wear or age. Older roofs may be on an actual cash value policy that pays out depreciated value rather than full replacement. Read your policy's roof section before you file, and document damage with photos.
