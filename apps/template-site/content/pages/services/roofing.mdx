    heading: |
      What a *real roof inspection* actually checks
    intro: 'A roof is a system, not a layer of shingles. Here is what our roofers look at on every inspection, and why each piece matters.'
    body: |
      The shingles are the part everyone notices, but they are only the outermost layer of a system that has to keep water out for thirty years. Underneath the shingles are the underlayment, the decking, and the ice and water shield, plus flashing at every penetration. A proper inspection starts in the attic, not on the roof, because water damage shows up on the underside of the decking before it shows up in your ceiling.

      From there we walk the roof itself. Shingle condition is the obvious part. The less obvious parts, and the ones that separate a real roofer from a sales rep, are flashing around the chimney and dormers, sealed pipe boots, undamaged drip edge, an intact ridge cap, and properly cut valleys. Any one of them failing puts the rest of the system under stress, and several of them failing is how a roof ends up needing [emergency tarp service](/services/emergency-repairs) after the next storm.

      Ventilation is the part most homeowners never hear about. Guidance from the [International Code Council](https://www.iccsafe.org/) and most major shingle manufacturers requires balanced intake and exhaust to keep shingles within their rated temperature range. Bad ventilation cuts shingle life nearly in half. Our inspection report covers ventilation, not just surfaces, because skipping it is how a roof needs replacement years before it should.

    heading: |
      Inspect the *whole roof*, not just the visible spot
    body: |
      A leak in the ceiling is usually the loudest problem in the roofing system, not the only one. By the time you call, there is often a section of decking starting to soften, a chimney flashing that lost its seal three storms ago, and a valley that is on its way out next.

      Our roofers walk the whole system before they quote anything. We start in the attic, where the actual damage shows up first, then move to the roof. We check decking, flashing, underlayment, ventilation, and the lifecycle stage of the shingles or membrane. By the end of the visit you have a written read on what is failing now, what is borderline, and what is fine.

      A few things shape every install:

      * Flat-rate pricing in writing before any work begins
      * Tear-off and decking inspection on every full replacement, no roof-overs unless you specifically ask
      * Ice and water shield in valleys, around penetrations, and along eaves where code requires
      * Written workmanship warranty on top of the manufacturer warranty on the materials
      * Cleanup that includes a magnet sweep and a final walkthrough you sign off on

      This is the standard we hold every roofing job to. Skipping these steps is how homeowners end up doing the same roof twice in fifteen years, and we are not the crew that does that.

        answer: |
          For a typical residential asphalt shingle roof, tear-off, decking inspection, and reinstall usually take **one to three days** depending on size, pitch, and weather.       - question: How do I know if I should repair or replace my roof?
        answer: |
          The honest answer is: it depends on the age of the roof, the type of damage, and how much of the system has reached end of life. A single damaged area on a five-year-old roof is almost always a repair. A leak on a twenty-year-old roof with curling shingles and exposed nail heads is usually a sign the whole system has hit retirement. Our inspection report lays out both numbers so you can compare them honestly.

        answer: |

The exact scope is spelled out in writing before the job starts.
