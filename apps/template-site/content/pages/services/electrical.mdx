    label: Why code matters
    heading: |
      What *current electrical code* exists to prevent
    intro: 'Code is not bureaucracy. It is what residential electrical work has learned the hard way over the last hundred years.'
    body: |
      Every revision of the [National Electrical Code](https://www.nfpa.org/codes-and-standards/nfpa-70-standard-development/70) is written in response to something that went wrong in real homes. Arc-fault breakers exist because a generation of bedroom fires traced back to nicked wiring behind drywall. Tamper-resistant outlets exist because of decades of pediatric injury data. Bonding and grounding rules exist because of the shock and surge events that destroyed homeowner electronics for years.

      A code-compliant install matters even when nobody is checking. A panel that meets current code protects against the specific failure modes documented in the field. A panel that does not, even if it appears to work today, is one fault condition away from a problem that should have been caught at install. Our electricians work from the current adopted code in your jurisdiction, pull permits when the scope requires them, and ground and bond every install the way the code intends.

      The flip side: a lot of older homes have wiring that is grandfathered, not actually compliant. Knob-and-tube, ungrounded two-prong outlets, mixed aluminum and copper at devices, federal panel breakers known to fail to trip. Grandfathering is not a guarantee of safety. Our [inspection report](/contact) tells you in plain English what is grandfathered, what is borderline, and what to address before it becomes an [emergency call](/services/emergency-repairs).

      Frequently asked *questions*

    faqs:
      - question: How do I know if my panel needs an upgrade?
        answer: |
          Common signs include breakers that trip when major appliances run, lights that dim when the AC kicks on, only having 100 amps of service in a home with modern loads, and visible scorch marks or rust inside the panel. Federal Pacific, Zinsco, and certain Pushmatic panels are also flagged in the industry as worth replacing on age and reliability grounds alone. We can give you an honest read after a free on-site evaluation.
      - question: Do you pull permits for electrical work?
        answer: |
          Yes for any work that requires one. Panel upgrades, new circuits, service changes, and most installs are permitted in your name and inspected by the local authority. This protects your home insurance and your ability to sell cleanly later. Skipping the permit to save a few hundred dollars usually costs far more on the back end.
      - question: Can you install an EV charger at my house?
        answer: |
          Yes. We install Level 2 home chargers on a properly sized dedicated circuit, with a breaker matched to the unit and a permit pulled where required. The visit usually starts with a quick panel load check to confirm there is enough capacity, or to scope a sub-panel if there is not.
      - question: How long does a panel upgrade usually take?
        answer: |
          A typical residential panel upgrade is a one-day job once the materials are on hand, plus a separate inspection visit. Power is off for several hours during the swap. We coordinate with the utility for service disconnect and reconnect so the timeline is predictable.
      - question: What should I do if my outlet is sparking or smells hot?
        answer: |
          Turn the breaker off for that circuit and call us right away. Sparking, scorching, and that distinctive "hot plastic" smell are all signs of a serious issue, not a minor annoyance. We treat those as emergencies and aim to have a technician on site the same day.
      - question: Do you stand behind your work with a warranty?
        answer: |
          Every electrical install is backed by a written workmanship warranty in addition to the manufacturer warranty on any panels, breakers, or fixtures we install. If something we touched fails inside the warranty window, we come back and make it right.
