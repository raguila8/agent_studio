    heading: |
      What a *real plumbing and HVAC diagnosis* looks like
    intro: 'A diagnosis is more than a guess on the phone. Here is what our technicians actually do on site before quoting any repair.'
    body: |
      A diagnosis on a no-heat call is not flipping the thermostat. It is checking gas pressure at the valve, reading flame current at the ignitor, pulling the inducer motor amp draw, and looking at static pressure across the duct system. Each reading rules in or out a different root cause, and skipping them is how a $300 fix becomes a $3,000 callback.

      The same logic applies to plumbing. A slow drain is often a vent issue, not a clog. A water heater that "runs out of hot water faster than it used to" is usually a sediment problem, a dip-tube failure, or a thermostat that drifted. Industry standards from groups like the [Air Conditioning Contractors of America](https://www.acca.org/) and current local code give technicians a clear playbook for how each kind of failure should be diagnosed.

      Our crews work from that playbook, not from instinct. The readings get written down and shared with you. If your system needs a repair, you see the numbers that justify it. If it does not, the visit goes on the record toward a [maintenance plan](/services/maintenance-plans) instead, or stays on file in case it ever turns into an [after-hours call](/services/emergency-repairs).

    heading: |
      Diagnose the *whole system*, not just the symptom
    body: |
      A water heater that quit last night is usually only the loudest problem in the room. By the time you call, there is often a slow drip behind it, a thermostat working harder than it should, and a furnace one component away from a no-heat call of its own.

      Our plumbing and HVAC technicians walk the whole system before they quote anything. We test water pressure, check static pressure on the duct system, inspect drains, look at flue draft, and pull readings off the components that actually matter. By the end of the visit you have a written read on what is failing now, what is on its way out, and what is fine.

      A few things shape every visit:

      * Flat-rate pricing in writing before any work begins
      * Same-day repairs whenever the parts and schedule allow
      * Permits pulled for any work that requires them
      * Written workmanship warranty on top of manufacturer coverage
      * Plain-English explanations of every recommendation, with the numbers behind them

      This is the standard we hold every plumbing and HVAC call to. Not because it is a marketing line, but because it is what keeps homeowners calling us back for the next job and the one after that.
